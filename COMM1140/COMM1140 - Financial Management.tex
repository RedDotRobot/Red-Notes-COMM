\section{Introduction to Financial Management}
	\textbf{Learning Objectives}
		\begin{itemize}
			\item Differentiate between accounting, finance, and tax
			\item Understand the different forms of accounting information
			\item Differentiate between revenues and expenses
			\item Explain the concepts of accrual accounting, cash accounting, and accounting profit
		\end{itemize}
	
	\subsection{What is financial management?}
		\begin{itemize}
			\item Financial management is the process of planning, organising, controlling, and monitoring financial resources to achieve company goals and objectives $\rightarrow$ how we use information to make informed business decisions
			\item The \textit{primary objective} of financial management is to maximise shareholder value through appropriate utilisation and decision making
		\end{itemize}

	\subsection{Why is financial management important to study}
		\begin{itemize}
			\item Financial management is a key life skill
			\item Entrepreneurship and roles within a company
			\item Implications on all aspects of business operations (such as Marketing, Management, Tax, Information Systems, Actuary)
			\item Eg. Budgeting within a business
			\item Measuring performance of a business, eg. profit
		\end{itemize}

		\textbf{Accounting is how you understand and run a successful business}

		Signs of business health include
			\begin{itemize}
				\item Profitability
				\item Cash flow
				\item Short and long term sustainability
				\item Business value
			\end{itemize}
		
		Accounting powers every business decision, categorised as long-term (strategic), short-term (operational), commercial (evaluation of business deals). Ultimately, accounting enables career success, as an advisor, entrepreneur, or a leader. Accounting is a good pathway into a career, as a way of entering a company. \\
		
		Finance helps you value a business and make good investment decisions using accounting information.

			\item Accounting ensures that high-quality numbers are available for analysis
			\item Finance needs accounting to begin the process of \colorbox{pastel_green}{valuation} (ie. to estimate the value or price of companies/assets/new projects)
		\end{itemize}

	\subsection{Accounting and Tax}
		\begin{itemize}
			\item Tax is the regulatory guidebook that ensures financial responsibility and compliance for a successful business
			\item It involves understanding and applying tax laws to ensure compliance, minimise tax liabilities, and take advantage of available deductions
			\item Tax calculations draw directly on accounting information
			\item It is an important part of financial management in minimising liabilities, leverage deductions, and contributing ethically to government revenue
		\end{itemize}
	\subsection{Accounting Information}
		\subsubsection{Who uses accounting information?}
			\begin{itemize}
				\item A wide range of stakeholders use accounting information daily
				\item These users often have different needs that accounting information can satisfy
			\end{itemize}

		\subsubsection{What are the different types of information in accounting}
			\begin{itemize}
				\item Financial accounting $\rightarrow$ Information given to people \colorbox{pastel_red}{outside} of the enterprise
				\item Management accounting $\rightarrow$ Information given to users \colorbox{pastel_blue}{within} the enterprise to assist in operational planning and controlling decisions
				\item Audit and internal control $\rightarrow$ An audit confirms the accuracy of financial statements and internal control involves implementing policies and procedures that safeguard assets and ensure reliable financial information
				\item Social and environmental accounting (accounting for sustainability) $\rightarrow$ Provision of non-financial information to users \colorbox{pastel_red}{external} to the enterprise 
			\end{itemize}

		\subsubsection{Financial accounting information and decision-making}
			\begin{itemize}
				\item \textbf{Financial accounting} primarily serves \colorbox{pastel_red}{external stakeholders}, such as investors/shareholders, creditors, regulatory bodies and the general public
				\item It provides an overview of a company's financial health
				\item Analysing financial statements allows managers to evaluate profitability, liquidity, and solvency
				\item Investors rely on financial statements to inform their investment decisions
			\end{itemize}

		\subsubsection{Management accounting information and decision-making}
			\begin{itemize}
				\item \textbf{Management accounting} is designed for \colorbox{pastel_blue}{internal stakeholders}, including managers and executives
				\item It provides detailed financial information to aid in strategic planning, budgeting, performance evaluation, and internal decision-making
				\item Cost accounting, a subset of management accounting, allows managers to optimise pricing and cost efficiency
			\end{itemize}

		\subsubsection{Audit and internal control information and decision-making}
			\begin{itemize}
				\item \textbf{Audits and internal control systems} ensure the reliability and integrity of financial inform, instilling confidence in decision-makers
				\item Audits and internal control systems can also benefit internal stakeholders by safeguarding assets and promoting operational efficiency
				\item Investors trust audited financial statements
			\end{itemize}

		\subsubsection{Social and environmental accounting information and decision-making}
			\begin{itemize}
				\item Social and environmental accounting caters to both internal and external stakeholders
				\item Internally, it helps management assess the company's impact on environmental and societal sustainability
				\item Externally, it informs socially conscious investors, customers, and regulatory bodies about the company's commitment to social and environmental responsibility
			\end{itemize}


	\subsection{Revenue and Expenses}

		\subsubsection{Revenue}
			\begin{itemize}
				\item Revenue represents an increase in company wealth
				\item Wealth increases because customers:
					\begin{itemize}
						\item Pay cash for goods or services
						\item Promise to pay cash (accounts receivable)
					\end{itemize}
				\item Types of revenue include:
					\begin{itemize}
						\item Sales revenue $\rightarrow$ Generated by selling goods and/or services in the ordinary course of business
						\item Other revenue may consist of items such as:
							\begin{itemize}
								\item Interest income on bank accounts or investments (passive revenue)
								\item Dividends received from investments in other companies (dividends are optional payments to shareholders from the profit of the company as a "thank-you" payment)
							\end{itemize}
					\end{itemize}
			\end{itemize}

		\subsubsection{Expenses}

			\begin{itemize}
				\item Expenses represent decreases in company wealth
				\item Expenses must be incurred in order to earn revenue
				\item These \textbf{do not} include the payment of dividends since these are "distributions" of net profit and are \colorbox{pastel_red}{not} mandatory
			\end{itemize}

	\subsection{Cash Accounting}
		\begin{itemize}
			\item Cash accounting involves recording revenues and expenses \textbf{at the time the cash is received or paid}
			\item This is reasonably precise given that the accountant knows whether cash has been paid or received, and the amount is easily determined
		\end{itemize}

		\subsubsection{Limitations of cash accounting}
			\begin{itemize}
				\item The complexity of business means that the financial health of a company is affected by transactions and cash flows in both the past and future
				\item Events where the \textbf{timing} of cash flows differs from the \textbf{substance} of the transaction include:
					\begin{itemize}
						\item Sale of goods or services on credit
						\item Services that will be paid in a later period (eg. electricity, water, gas, hotel accommodations)
						\item Companies receiving cash in advance for services provided (eg. magazine subscriptions, airline tickets, concert tickets)
					\end{itemize}
			\end{itemize}

	\subsection{Accrual Accounting}
		\begin{itemize}
			\item To cope with these complexities, most businesses use accrual accounting
			\item Accrual accounting involves recording revenues and expenses \textbf{at the time they occur}, not when the cash is received or paid
			\item In the income statement, revenues and expenses are recognised when an economically meaningful event has occurred
			\item The \colorbox{pastel_green}{key test for revenue recognition is whether the goods and services have been \textbf{rendered}} (ie. delivery to a customer or provision of a service)
			\item \textbf{Receipt of cash is not required for revenue to be recognised}
			\item Equipment used for multiple years is not considered an expense, rather an asset. The depreciation of the asset is the expense
		\end{itemize}

			\centering
			\setstretch{1.25}
			\begin{tabular}{p{4cm}|p{6cm}|p{6cm}}
				\hline
				\multicolumn{1}{c|}{} & 
				\multicolumn{1}{c|}{\textbf{Cash basis}} & 
				\multicolumn{1}{c}{\textbf{Accrual basis}} \\
				\hline
				\textbf{Record revenues when:} & Cash is received 	& Revenues are earned \\
				\textbf{Record expenses when:} & Cash is paid		& Expenses are incurred \\
				\textbf{In Australia, the ATO says that:}			& Businesses with a turnover of less than \$10 million can use cash accounting		& Businesses with a turnover of more than \$10 million must use accrual accounting \\
				\textbf{Benefits:}									& Simple and effective in managing the cash flow position of a business				& More accurate and clear view of the financial health of the business \\
				\hline
			\end{tabular}
		\end{table}

\section{Understanding Financial Statements}
	
	\textbf{Learning Objectives}
		\begin{itemize}
			\item Understand how accounting information is presented through the financial statements
			\item Describe the relationship between the balance sheet and the income statement (P \& L)
			\item Identify the components of the income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statement
			\item Prepare in income statement and balance sheet
		\end{itemize}

	\subsection{How is financial accounting information presented}
		\begin{itemize}
			\item Financial accounting information is presented in an annual report
			\item The annual report is a comprehensive document produced by a company at the end of its financial year
			\item It serves as a detailed account of the company's financial performance, operational activities, and overall position during the preceding year
			\item Annual reports can be obtained from either
				\begin{itemize}
					\item Company websites
					\item Stock exchanges
				\end{itemize}
		\end{itemize}

	\subsection{Key financial statements}
		\begin{itemize}
			\item Balance sheet
				\begin{itemize}
					\item Financial position of a company \colorbox{pastel_red}{at a particular point in time}
					\item Company's set of financial resources and obligations at a point in time
					\item It shows a company's resources (assets) and how they were financed (liability + equity)
				\end{itemize}
			\item Income statement
				\begin{itemize}
					\item Financial performance of a company over a period of time
					\item How effect a company is at generating a profit from operations \colorbox{pastel_red}{over a period of time}
					\item It shows the revenues and expenses incurred over a period of time
				\end{itemize}
			\item Cash flow statement
				\begin{itemize}
					\item Measures cash inflows (sources of cash) and cash outflows (uses of cash) \colorbox{pastel_red}{over a period of time}
					\item Prepared using cash accounting
				\end{itemize}
		\end{itemize}

		\subsubsection{The Balance Sheet}
			\begin{itemize}
				\item In Australia, the end of the financial year is 30 June since it aligns with the tax year end
				\item The balance sheet has three main elements:
					\begin{itemize}
						\item Assets
							\begin{itemize}
								\item Resources that will be used by the company this year or in future years
								\item Eg. cash, accounts receivable, property, equipment, inventory, investments
								\item Classified as:
									\begin{itemize}
										\item \colorbox{pastel_green}{Current} (short-term) assets expected to be used in the next 12 months, eg. cash, accounts receivable, inventory
										\item \colorbox{pastel_green}{Non-current} (long-term) assets that will be used for longer than 12 months, eg. property, plant and equipment, buildings, land
									\end{itemize}
							\end{itemize}
						\item Liabilities
							\begin{itemize}
								\item A list of debt and other financial obligations (what the company owes)
								\item Eg. accounts payable, loan payable, income taxes payable
								\item Classified as:
									\begin{itemize}
										\item \colorbox{pastel_green}{Current,} eg. accounts payable, wages payable
										\item \colorbox{pastel_green}{Non-current} liabilities that will extend passed a year, eg. loans payable
									\end{itemize}
							\end{itemize}
						\item Shareholder's Equity (also known as Owner's Equity)
							\begin{itemize}
								\item What belongs to the owners, the "net assets" of the company (what would be left if all liabilities were paid for and all assets sold \colorbox{pastel_purple}{($SE = A-L$))}
								\item Consists of:
									\begin{itemize}
										\item Share capital
										\item Retained profits
									\end{itemize}
							\end{itemize}
					\end{itemize}
				\item \colorbox{pastel_red}{Note:} the sum of assets on a Balance Sheet must always equal the sum of total liabilities and shareholder's equity
			\end{itemize}

			\begin{question}{Example}
				\begin{align*}
					\text{Assets} &= \text{Liabilities} + \text{Equity} 			\\
					\textit{(Resources)} &\qquad \textit{(Sources)}					\\
					\$30,000 &= \$18,000 + \$12,000									\\
							 & \text{Bought furniture for \$10,000 with a loan}		\\
					\$10,000 &= \$10,000											\\
					\$40,000 &= \$28,000 + \$12,000									\\
							 & \text{Or}											\\
					\text{Assets} &- \text{Liabilities} = \text{Equity}				\\
					\$40,000 &- \$28,000 = \$12,000
				\end{align*}
			\end{question}
		
		\subsubsection{Income Statement}
			\begin{itemize}
				\item Displays financial performance derived from business operations over a specific time period
					\begin{itemize}
						\item Reports revenues earned, and any expenses incurred
						\item \textbf{Revenue}: inflows of economic benefits that increase Shareholder's Equity
						\item \textbf{Expenses}: Use or loss of economic benefits that decrease Shareholder's Equity
					\end{itemize}
			\end{itemize}

				You give a lecture in February for \$3,000. You receive the cash in March. You pay \$1,000, in February, to hire the room.

		\subsubsection{Statement of Cash Flows}
			\begin{itemize}
				\item The cash flow statement provides details of movements in a company's cash balance over a specific time period
				\item These are categorised into:
					\begin{enumerate}
						\item \textbf{Operating activities:} main revenue-producing activities
						\item \textbf{Investing activities:} acquisition and disposal of long-term assets
						\item \textbf{Financing activities:} equity capital and borrowings
					\end{enumerate}
				\item Prepared using cash accounting
			\end{itemize}

	\subsection{Connecting the Income Statement and the Balance Sheet}
		\begin{itemize}
			\item \textbf{Retained profits} represent the sum of all previous profits minus any dividends paid to shareholders
				\begin{itemize}
					\item Dividends are a distribution of the profits as a reward for investing in the company
				\end{itemize}
			\item Share capital is defined as the money invested in the business in return for shares
		\end{itemize}

\section{Analysing Business Transactions}

	\textbf{Learning Objectives}

		At the end of this topic, you will be able to:
		\begin{enumerate}
			\item Identify and record business transactions using transaction analysis
			\item Understand how different business transactions impact the financial statements
			\item Identify how accrual adjustments impact the financial statements
		\end{enumerate}

	\subsection{What are business transactions?}

		\begin{itemize}
			\item Transactions are events that affect the operations or finances of an organisation
				\begin{itemize}
					\item Examples: selling goods or services, paying employees, paying dividends
				\end{itemize}
			\item Transaction analysis involves examining each business business transaction with the aim of understanding its effect on the accounting equation ($A = L + SE$)
		\end{itemize}

	\subsection{Recording transactions using the accounting equation}

		\begin{question}{Example}
			\textbf{Revenues + Expenses}

				\underline{In March:}
				\begin{enumerate}
					\item You give a training course for \$5,000 in cash
					\item You pay \$1,000 rent for the room
				\end{enumerate}
				\begin{alignat*}{2}
					\text{Assets} \quad &= \quad \text{Liabilities} \quad &&+ \quad \text{Equity}	\\
					\text{Cash } + 5,000 \quad &= &&+ \quad 5,000	\\
					\text{Cash } (1,000) \quad &= && \qquad (1,000)
				\end{alignat*}

				Hence, profit is \$4,000.
				\\

			\textbf{Equity} 

				\begin{table}[H]
					\begin{center}
						\setstretch{1.25}
						\begin{tabular}[c]{p{8cm}|p{3cm}}
							\hline
							Statement of Retained Profit				& \$		\\
							\hline
							Opening Retained Profit (1st March)			& 10,000	\\
							\qquad Profit 								& 4,000		\\
							\qquad - Dividends							& (3,000)	\\
							Closing Retained Profit (31st March)		& 11,000	\\
							\hline
						\end{tabular}
					\end{center}
				\end{table}
			
		\end{question}

		\begin{alignat*}{3}
			\text{Assets} \quad &= \quad \text{Liabilities} \quad &&+ \quad \text{Shareholder's Equity}	\\
			A \quad &= \quad L \quad &&+ \quad SE	\\
			CA + NCA \quad &= \quad CL + NCL \quad &&+ \quad SE
		\end{alignat*}

		Where:
		\begin{itemize}
			\item $CA + NCA = \text{Current Assets} + \text{Non-Current Assets}$
			\item $CL + NCL = \text{Current Liabilities} + \text{Non-Current Liabilities}$
			\item $SE = SC + Op. \; RP + \text{Profits} - \text{Dividends}$
				\begin{itemize}
					\item $SC = \text{Capital contributions by equity holders (Share Capital)}$
					\item $Op. \; RP = \text{Opening Retained Profits}$
					\item $\text{Profit} = \text{Revenues} - \text{Expenses}$
				\end{itemize}
		\end{itemize}

	\subsection{Accrual Accounting Adjustments}

		\begin{itemize}
			\item Accrual accounting requires revenues and expenses to be recognised in the accounting period to which they relate (ie. when they occur)
			\item This may require an accrual adjustment to be made on the last day of the financial period to bring records up to date
		\end{itemize}

		\subsubsection{Unearned revenues (deferred revenues)}

			\begin{itemize}
				\item You are a Seller (receiver of cash)
				\item Cash is received in advance of earning revenue
					\begin{itemize}
						\item The recognition of revenue is deferred
					\end{itemize}
				\item \colorbox{pastel_red}{A liability account}
				\item Liability becomes revenue when goods or services owing are provided
				\item Examples
					\begin{itemize}
						\item Cash deposit for work to be carried out in the future
						\item Airline and concert tickets
						\item Video streaming subscriptions
					\end{itemize}
			\end{itemize}

		\subsubsection{Prepayments}
		
			\begin{itemize}
				\item You are a Customer (cash payer)
				\item Cash paid in advance of incurring expense
					\begin{itemize}
						\item There is still value extending into the future
					\end{itemize}
				\item Examples
					\begin{itemize}
						\item Prepaid insurance
						\item Prepaid rent
						\item Office supplies
					\end{itemize}
				\item \colorbox{pastel_green}{An asset account}
				\item Once the asset is used, the expense is incurred and an adjustment is made
			\end{itemize}

		\subsubsection{Accrued revenues (receivables)}

			\begin{itemize}
				\item You are a Seller
				\item Revenue has been earned, but cash not received
				\item \colorbox{pastel_green}{An asset account}
				\item Examples
					\begin{itemize}
						\item Credit sales
						\item Commissions earned but not received
						\item Interest earned on an investment but not received
					\end{itemize}
			\end{itemize}

		\subsubsection{Accrued expenses (payables)}

			\begin{itemize}
				\item You are a Customer
				\item Expenses are incurred, but the cash is not paid
				\item \colorbox{pastel_red}{A liability account}
				\item Examples
					\begin{itemize}
						\item Wages earned by employees but not paid
						\item Interest payable on outstanding loan
						\item Bills for electricity, gas, or water usage not paid
					\end{itemize}
			\end{itemize}

	\subsection{Depreciation of non-current assets}
		
		\begin{itemize}
			\item Depreciation involves allocating the cost of a non-current asset over its lifespan (useful like) to acknowledge the gradual consumption of the asset's economic value as an expense
			\item Accumulated Depreciation (Balance Sheet account) shows all depreciation charged against an asset to date
			\item Depreciation Expense (Income Statement account) shows only this year's depreciation allocation
		\end{itemize}

		\begin{question}{Example}
			\begin{itemize}
				\item Equipment costs \$10,000 with a useful life of 4 years and no estimated salvage value
				\item The \textbf{straight-line depreciation method} is applied throughout the equipment's useful life, reflecting the assumption that the asset will be utilised evenly over its lifespan
				\item $\text{Depreciation calculation} = \$10,000 / 4 \text{ years } = \$2,500$
				\item After 3 years, the book value of the equipment is:
			\end{itemize}
			\begin{table}[H]
				\begin{center}
					\setstretch{1.25}
					\begin{tabular}[c]{p{8cm}p{3cm}}
						Equipment							& \$10,000	\\
						Less: Accumulated depreciation		& (\$7,500)	\\
						Book value							& \$2,500	\\
					\end{tabular}
				\end{center}
			\end{table}
		\end{question}
